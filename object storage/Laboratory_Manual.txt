Introduction\n\nNaturally occurring organic carbon (C) forms are derived from the decomposition of plants and animals. In\nsoils and sediments, a wide variety of organic carbon forms are present and range from freshly deposited litter\n(e.g. Leaves, twigs, branches, and roots) to highly decomposed forms such as humus. Soils may also contain\ncarbon in inorganic form, e.g. as carbonate minerals. Inert forms of elemental carbon from charred organic\ncarbon (black carbon) from forest fires or prescribed burning, coal, graphite, carbides, or soot particles\ndeposited from incompletely combusted fossil fuels may occur in soils.\n\nForests sequester and store more carbon aboveground than any other terrestrial ecosystem. Part of the carbon\nstore is in the organic humus layer of soil, but also deeper in the soil profile, driven by the infiltrating rain\nwater. Soil organic carbon (SOC) constitutes a significant part of the forest carbon store. Where litter falls in\nwater saturated environments and is deposited as peat, soil organic carbon stores may greatly exceed those in\nthe aboveground biomass. Soil organic matter is also a store of nutrients such as nitrogen (N) and phosphorus\n\n(P).\n\nMangrove forests are among the most productive ecosystems in the world. A major part of the organic carbon\nstock is found belowground in mangroves. The sediment has high salinity and is largely water saturated,\nthereby preserving the organic residues of litter, debris, and dead roots. Mangrove sediments have high\nchloride content as well as high organic content, but also may include significant amounts of carbonates from\nthe underlying coral reefs, which all affect the analytical procedures of TOC.\n\nTotal carbon (TC) in a soil sample includes both inorganic and organic constituents. Inorganic carbon can be\npresent from calcareous parent materials and is not subject of this Manual. Total organic carbon (TOC) can be\ndetermined from dried and acidified soil samples using dry or wet combustion methods.\n\nThere are challenges in preserving the samples, transporting them to laboratory, storing and analyzing them\nwithout a systematic error following from these procedures. Another challenge is the presence of inorganic\ncarbon in form of carbonates derived from the parent materials that can severely disturb the determination of\nTOC, as carbonate minerals dissolve in acidic treatment directly adding to the analysis estimate of TOC. It is\nthus desirable to have a simple, rapid and accurate method for the analysis of soil organic carbon.\n\nA traditional dry combustion method based on ignition mass loss of oven-dried samples is unselective due to\nloss of carbonate minerals and is time consuming. Modern dry combustion methods based on detection of CO\nrelease from the heated sample can provide automated analysis sequences with help of peak integration\nsoftware and sample autoloader to save human labour. Unfortunately, the determination of soil carbon content\nby automated dry combustion methods requires the use of costly and specialized equipment. Wet combustion\nusing dichromate sulphuric acid (Walkley-Black method, originally published by Walkley& Black, 1934)\nrequires traditional manual analytics, applies poisonous and harmful chemicals, but can yield to a reasonable\ndaily throughput of samples with moderate costs and commonly available laboratory equipment.\n\nCarbonate containing soils cause difficulties in TOC determination in dry combustion methods. Elimination of\nthe carbonate effect from TOC is hard, because some organic Carbon (OC) compounds may be lost in acid\ntreatment when carbonate is removed, leading to 10-30% error in TOC estimate in some cases. Even when an\naccurate TOC result may not be obtained in samples rich of carbonates it is imperative to know of the\ncarbonate effect not to overestimate soil OC. Carbonates do not interfere significantly with the dichromate wet\ncombustion method. Thus Walkley-Black Wet Oxidation method can be a solution for calcareous soils.\nWalkley-Black is applicable when enough laboratory personnel is available. However, the Walkley-Black\nresults are known to be subject to uncertainties since only the easily combusted OC fractions are oxidized, and\nthe results must be corrected using a proper correction factor. Literature derived correction factors are often\nused, but better results could be obtained using specific comparisons with other methods for each soil type.\n\nThis Manual aims at describing methods for reasonably accurate TOC analyses that are compatible with other\nresults obtained in the region. Different methods are presented for upland soil samples and\nmangrove sediment samples. In situ\nthey are also anoxic or hypoxic, the condition which reduces decomposition rate of organic matter. When the\nsamples are taken, they are exposed to oxic conditions. The procedures of sampling attempt to ensure that the\nsediment samples are packed as air-tightly as possible. In order to preserve the samples optimally, they should\nbe kept in cold, dark container and transported to laboratory as soon as possible. A cooler box with ice could\nprovide such conditions, but when the samples are transported from coast to upcountry laboratories, the\ncapacity of the cooler box may not be adequate. Therefore, the samples may be exposed to high temperatures\nover varying periods of time after they have been extracted from the mangrove sites.", "When the samples arrive, they should be immediately identified, their ID and location information entered in\nthe specific mangrove excel workbook, and without delay prepared for oven drying. If it is not possible start\nthe drying immediately, the samples can be kept refrigerated for a short period of time (1-2 days), or they can\nbe stored in a freezer at -20\u00b0C for longer periods of time.\n\n3.2 Processing of mangrove sediment samples\n\nOven drying\n\nAluminium foil is cut and folded in a shape so that it adequately holds the about 1.9 dl sediment sample. A\ncup-like form is preferable, because that will optimize the oven capacity. The dry aluminium \u201ccup\u201d is first\nweighed and its mass marked on the cup.\n\nThe sediment sample is taken carefully from its plastic bag and quantitatively transferred to the aluminium\ncup. The sample ID information from the masking tape tag on the plastic bag has to follow the sample during\nits processing. Make sure no material is lost during the transfer and all materials from the plastic bag are\ntaken. Place the samples directly to the oven at temperature of \u201460\u00b0C. The drying process may take several\ndays until all removable water has evaporated, and the samples have reached constant mass.\n\nWhen the sample has been dried to constant mass in the cup of known mass, the samples will be weighed for\ndetermining of bulk density using a laboratory table-top scale (not analytical scale). Then the mass of the\naluminium cup is subtracted from the total mass. Because the samples tend to adsorb a considerable amount of\nwater from air humidity as soon as they are taken from the oven, it is advised that the samples are weighed at\nimmediate vicinity of the oven, and taken one by one from the oven directly to the scale. Since the samples are\nhot, the scale has to be protected using a heat-insulating plate on the scale. The mass of the plate has to be\nsubtracted (tare weight) so that the scale reading is zero with the plate on the scale) prior to putting the sample\non the heat-resistant plate. The weighing results can now be recorded in the mangrove excel application.\n\n3.3 Homogenization of mangrove sediment samples\n\nThe dried samples are carefully homogenized using a mortar. Any root can be picked out and discarded. The\nhomogenised samples are sieved using a 2 mm sieve to remove roots and other large particles, after which\nthey can further be sieved using a 0.5 mm for improved homogeneity.
